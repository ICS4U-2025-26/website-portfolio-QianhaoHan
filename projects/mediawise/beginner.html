Module 1

Media Literacy Basics

Learn what media literacy means and how media messages shape what people think, feel, and do.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: What Media Literacy Means

Media literacy means being able to understand, question, and evaluate the media you see. This includes news articles, social media posts, videos, images, memes, podcasts, charts, advertisements, and AI-generated content.

Being media literate does not mean distrusting everything. It means learning how to pause and ask useful questions before accepting something as true.

Learning Goals

  • Explain what media literacy means.
  • Understand that misleading content is not always completely false.
  • Recognize how media can inform, entertain, persuade, or sell.

Lesson 2: Different Types of Media

Media can appear as news articles, social media posts, ads, blogs, videos, podcasts, memes, screenshots, charts, and AI-generated responses. Each type of media has its own purpose and style.

A news article might aim to inform, while an advertisement aims to persuade you to buy something. A meme may be funny, but it can still spread a political or social message.

Questions to Ask

  • What type of media is this?
  • Who created it?
  • What does the creator want me to think, feel, or do?
  • Is it trying to inform, entertain, persuade, or sell?

Lesson 3: Target Audience and Purpose

Every media message is made for an audience. Creators choose words, images, sounds, examples, and headlines that appeal to that audience.

For example, a video made for teenagers may use humor, fast editing, and popular music. A political ad may use serious music and emotional images to make an issue feel urgent.

Lesson 4: Emotional Language and Persuasion

Emotional language is often used to make people angry, afraid, excited, or shocked. Strong emotion does not automatically mean something is false, but it is a reason to slow down.

Loaded Words to Watch For

  • Greedy
  • Reckless
  • Lazy
  • Heroic
  • Radical
  • Betray
  • Attack
  • Disaster

Lesson 5: Fact, Opinion, and Entertainment

A fact can be checked. An opinion expresses a belief or judgment. Entertainment is designed mainly to amuse or engage an audience. Many media messages mix all three.

  • Fact: “The city council voted 8–3 to approve the policy.”
  • Opinion: “The policy is unfair and unnecessary.”
  • Interpretation: “The vote shows leaders are prioritizing environmental issues.”
  • Entertainment: A comedy video pretending to report news.
Example:

These headlines could describe the same event:

  1. “City Launches New Climate Plan”
  2. “City Finally Takes Bold Action on Climate”
  3. “City Punishes Families With Costly Climate Rules”

The first is neutral, the second suggests approval, and the third suggests anger. This is media framing.

Activity:

Decide whether each sentence is a fact, opinion, or interpretation:

  1. “The bill passed by a vote of 52–48.”
  2. “The reckless bill will hurt ordinary people.”
  3. “Supporters say the bill will reduce costs.”
  4. “The close vote shows the country is divided.”
Quick Check:

Which question is most useful when practicing media literacy?

Answer: “Who made this, and what evidence do they provide?”

Module 2

Misinformation Basics

Learn what misinformation and disinformation are, why they spread, and how clickbait works.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: Misinformation vs. Disinformation

Misinformation is false or inaccurate information that may be shared by mistake. Disinformation is false information shared on purpose to mislead people.

  • Misinformation: Someone shares an old photo because they think it is from today.
  • Disinformation: Someone knowingly posts a fake image to influence people.

Both can be harmful because people may believe them, act on them, or share them further.

Lesson 2: Common Examples Online

Misinformation can appear as fake screenshots, misleading headlines, old videos reposted as new, fake quotes, AI-generated images, edited graphs, or posts that leave out important context.

A post does not need to be completely fake to be misleading. Sometimes the problem is what the post leaves out.

Lesson 3: Why False Information Spreads Quickly

False information often spreads because it is emotional, surprising, simple, or confirms what people already believe. Social media platforms can also reward posts that get strong reactions, even when those posts are misleading.

Common Triggers

  • Fear
  • Anger
  • Shock
  • Excitement
  • A feeling that “this proves I was right”

Lesson 4: Clickbait and Misleading Headlines

Clickbait uses dramatic or incomplete headlines to make people click. A headline can be technically connected to a story but still exaggerate what the evidence shows.

Before reacting to a headline, read past it. Check whether the article actually supports the claim in the headline.

Lesson 5: Introduction to Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias happens when people pay more attention to information that supports what they already believe and ignore information that challenges them.

A useful question is: “Would I still believe this if it disagreed with my opinion?”

Example:

A post says, “This video proves the mayor hates students!” The clip is only 8 seconds long. The full speech is not linked. This may be misleading because the quote could be taken out of context.

Activity:

Find a headline online that seems dramatic. Ask: What emotion is it trying to create? Does the article provide evidence, or does it mainly rely on reaction?

Quick Check:

Why can false information spread quickly?

Answer: It often creates strong emotions and confirms what people already believe.

Module 3

Checking Sources

Learn how to identify trustworthy sources, check evidence, and cross-reference claims.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: What Is a Source?

A source is where information comes from. It might be a news article, government report, scientific study, company website, personal blog, social media post, screenshot, video, or AI-generated summary.

Before trusting a source, ask who created it, when it was published, what evidence it provides, and whether it is trying to sell or promote something.

Lesson 2: Identifying Trustworthy Websites and Authors

Stronger sources are transparent. They usually tell you who wrote the information, what organization published it, when it was published, and what evidence supports it.

Signs of a Stronger Source

  • A named author or organization
  • A clear publication date
  • Links to evidence
  • Expert input
  • Transparent methods
  • Corrections or update information
  • Clear ownership
  • A record of reliable reporting

Lesson 3: Checking Dates and Evidence

Old information can be misleading if it is presented as new. Always check when something was published and whether it links to original evidence.

Questions to Ask

  • When was this published?
  • Is the information still current?
  • Does the article link to evidence?
  • Can I find the original study, report, or document?

Lesson 4: Cross-Referencing Multiple Sources

Cross-referencing means checking whether other reliable sources report the same information. If only one unknown website is making a major claim, be careful.

For important claims, look for the original or primary source. Primary sources include government datasets, court documents, scientific studies, official reports, and full speeches.

Lesson 5: Fake Experts and Unreliable Claims

A fake expert may have no real credentials, no traceable background, or no connection to the topic. A conflict of interest happens when a person or organization may benefit from the conclusion they are presenting.

A company-funded study is not automatically false, but you should check whether the methods are fair and whether independent sources agree.

Lesson 6: Basic Fact-Checking Strategies

  • Search the claim in another tab.
  • Look for the original source.
  • Check the date and author.
  • Compare with trusted sources.
  • Look for fact-checking organizations that have covered it.
  • Do not rely on screenshots alone.
Activity:

Rank these sources from strongest to weakest for a claim about unemployment rates:

  1. National statistics agency
  2. Newspaper article summarizing official government data
  3. Influencer post with no citation
  4. Screenshot of a chart with no source
Quick Check:

Which source is usually strongest for checking a claim about a new law?

Answer: The official government website or legal document.

Module 4

AI Basics

Learn what AI is, where it appears in daily life, and how to recognize AI-generated content.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: What Artificial Intelligence Is

Artificial intelligence is technology that can perform tasks that usually require human thinking, such as recognizing patterns, answering questions, generating images, translating languages, or recommending content.

AI does not think exactly like a person. It uses patterns from data to make predictions, generate responses, or identify likely results.

Lesson 2: Everyday Examples of AI

  • Recommendation systems on YouTube, TikTok, Netflix, or Spotify
  • Chatbots and virtual assistants
  • Facial recognition tools
  • Autocorrect and translation tools
  • Search engines and personalized ads
  • AI image, video, or text generators

Lesson 3: Human-Created vs. AI-Generated Content

Some content is made by humans, some is made by AI, and some uses both. AI-generated content can look polished and realistic, but it may still contain mistakes.

Instead of only asking “Was this made by AI?” ask “Can I verify this information?”

Lesson 4: Benefits of AI

AI can help people brainstorm ideas, summarize information, translate text, organize notes, explain difficult topics, and create practice questions.

These uses can be helpful when AI supports learning rather than replacing your thinking.

Lesson 5: Limitations of AI

AI can produce inaccurate information, repeat bias, invent sources, or create realistic fake images and text.

Possible Warning Signs in AI Text

  • Very generic language
  • Few or no sources
  • Confident claims without evidence
  • Fake citations
  • Repeated sentence patterns

Possible Warning Signs in AI Images

  • Distorted hands or fingers
  • Unreadable background text
  • Strange reflections or shadows
  • Blurry background faces
  • No clear source or photographer
Activity:

Look at a piece of online content and ask: Could AI have helped create this? What evidence would I need before trusting it?

Quick Check:

Why is it not enough to say “this sounds like AI”?

Answer: Style alone does not prove AI authorship; you still need to check evidence and sources.

Module 5

Responsible AI Use

Learn ethical habits for using AI tools in school, daily life, and online media.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: Ethical AI Use

AI can be useful for brainstorming, explaining ideas, summarizing information, and practicing skills. But responsible AI use means being honest about how you use it and checking important information before trusting it.

  • Use AI as a helper, not a replacement for your thinking.
  • Check facts with reliable sources.
  • Follow your teacher’s or organization’s AI rules.
  • Do not use AI to deceive people.

Lesson 2: AI Can Be Inaccurate or Biased

AI tools can give answers that sound confident but are wrong. They can also repeat bias from the data they were trained on.

When AI gives an important claim, look for real sources. If the claim affects health, safety, schoolwork, money, or someone’s reputation, verify it carefully.

Lesson 3: Plagiarism and Schoolwork

It is dishonest to submit AI-generated work as if you created it entirely yourself. Better uses include asking AI to explain a concept, generate practice questions, or help brainstorm ideas before you write in your own words.

Lesson 4: Proper Use of AI Assistance

Responsible AI use means being clear about what parts of your work are yours and what parts were supported by AI. You should still understand the material and be able to explain your choices.

  • Use AI to study, not to cheat.
  • Rewrite ideas in your own words.
  • Check facts before including them.
  • Ask your teacher what is allowed.

Lesson 5: Critical Thinking With AI Media

When interacting with AI-generated media, ask whether the claims are specific, whether the sources are real, whether the evidence is strong, and whether the content might be missing important context.

A strong media-literate response sounds like: “This might be true, but I need better evidence before sharing it.”

Activity:

Imagine AI gives you a statistic for a school project. What steps should you take before using it? List at least three verification steps.

Quick Check:

Which is a responsible way to use AI?

Answer: Use AI to brainstorm, then check facts and write in your own words.
