Module 1

Advanced Source Evaluation

Analyze bias, tone, framing, reliability, influencers, podcasts, and how outlets cover the same story.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: Bias, Tone, and Framing

Advanced media literacy is not only about spotting fake information. Many misleading articles, posts, or videos contain facts. The problem is how those facts are selected, arranged, emphasized, or explained.

Framing is the angle or perspective used to present information. One headline might describe protesters as demanding justice, while another describes them as an angry mob. Both could describe the same event but create very different impressions.

Look For

  • Loaded or emotional words
  • Facts that are included or left out
  • Images that make one side look better or worse
  • Headlines that push a strong reaction

Lesson 2: Verifying Digital Content

Strong claims require strong evidence. A viral post, personal story, or dramatic example may feel convincing, but that does not mean it supports a broad conclusion.

Stronger Evidence Includes

  • Original documents
  • Large studies
  • Representative surveys
  • Transparent datasets
  • Peer-reviewed research
  • Multiple independent sources
  • Full video or transcript evidence

Ask whether the evidence actually supports the claim, whether the sample is large enough, and whether another explanation could exist.

Lesson 3: Evaluating Influencers, Podcasts, and Independent Media

Influencers and podcasts can explain issues clearly, but they may also have incentives: attention, sponsorships, political goals, personal branding, or product sales.

Check whether they cite sources, correct mistakes, interview qualified people, distinguish fact from opinion, and show enough context.

Lesson 4: Comparing Coverage Across Outlets

One of the best ways to understand framing is to compare how different outlets report the same story. Look at what each source emphasizes, what it minimizes, who it quotes, and what evidence it includes.

Questions to Ask

  • What details does each source emphasize?
  • What details are missing?
  • Are different experts or witnesses quoted?
  • Does the headline match the evidence?
Activity:

Choose two articles about the same event. Compare their headlines, quoted sources, emotional language, missing perspectives, and evidence.

Quick Check:

An article can be factually accurate but still misleading if it:

Answer: Selects only facts that support one interpretation.

Algorithms and Influence

Understand how algorithms, echo chambers, virality, targeted ads, and platforms shape opinions.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: How Algorithms Decide What You See

Algorithms are systems that decide what content appears in your feed, search results, recommendations, and ads. They often prioritize content that keeps users watching, clicking, liking, commenting, or sharing.

This means the content you see is not random. It is shaped by your past behavior, your interests, your location, platform goals, and what other users engage with.

Lesson 2: Echo Chambers and Filter Bubbles

A filter bubble happens when platforms mostly show content similar to what you already like. An echo chamber happens when you mostly hear opinions that reinforce your existing beliefs.

Why This Matters

  • You may see fewer opposing viewpoints.
  • Extreme opinions may seem more common than they are.
  • Misinformation can spread inside closed communities.
  • Your confidence can grow even when your information is incomplete.

Lesson 3: Engagement, Virality, and Information Spread

Platforms often reward posts that generate reactions. A post can go viral because it is emotional, shocking, funny, or controversial, not because it is accurate.

When you see viral content, ask whether it is spreading because it is well-supported or because it triggers a strong emotional response.

Lesson 4: Targeted Advertising and Personalized Content

Targeted ads are personalized based on information such as your searches, likes, location, age, interests, and online behavior.

When you see an ad, ask: “Why am I seeing this, and what does it want me to do?”

Lesson 5: How Platforms Influence Opinions and Behaviour

Platforms influence opinions by deciding what gets recommended, what gets hidden, what becomes trending, and what appears credible. This does not mean every platform is intentionally misleading you, but it does mean your feed is shaped by design choices.

Activity:

Open a social media feed or recommendation page. Write down three recommended posts. What do they have in common? Why might the platform have chosen them for you?

Quick Check:

What is a filter bubble?

Answer: When platforms mostly show content similar to what you already engage with.

Module 3

AI-Generated Media

Identify AI-generated images, videos, audio, articles, deepfakes, and synthetic media risks.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: What AI-Generated Media Includes

AI-generated media includes images, videos, voices, articles, comments, and posts created partly or entirely by artificial intelligence. Some of it is harmless or useful, but it can also be used to mislead.

  • AI-generated images
  • Deepfake videos
  • Fake audio clips
  • AI-written articles or comments
  • Synthetic social media profiles

Lesson 2: Identifying AI-Generated Articles and Text

AI-generated writing can sound polished and confident while still containing fake citations, invented experts, unsupported claims, or broken links.

Possible Red Flags

  • Generic explanations
  • Confident claims with no evidence
  • Fake or broken citations
  • Vague references to “experts”
  • No original reporting

Instead of asking only “Was this written by AI?” ask: “Are the claims, sources, and citations verifiable?”

Lesson 3: Deepfakes and Synthetic Media

A deepfake uses AI to make someone appear to say or do something they did not say or do. Deepfakes can be especially harmful when they involve public figures, elections, schools, emergencies, or accusations against people.

Warning Signs

  • Unnatural facial movement
  • Audio that does not match the mouth
  • Strange shadows or lighting
  • No reliable source confirming the clip
  • No full version available

Lesson 4: Detecting AI-Generated Images

Possible red flags include warped hands, strange teeth or eyes, blurry background faces, nonsensical text on signs, inconsistent reflections, mismatched shadows, repeated patterns, and missing source information.

These signs are not proof. Real photos can also contain motion blur, compression artifacts, strange lighting, or editing errors.

Lesson 5: Ethical Concerns Around AI-Created Media

AI-generated media raises ethical concerns because it can imitate real people, invent events, damage reputations, create fake evidence, or spread misinformation at scale.

Be especially careful with AI media involving real people, private individuals, emergencies, politics, health, or crime.

Activity:

A short video shows a public figure making a shocking statement, but the full video is unavailable. What would you check before believing it?

Quick Check:

What should a careful viewer do with a shocking short clip?

Answer: Look for the full original video and reputable reporting.

Module 4

Bias in Media and AI

Recognize representation, stereotypes, confirmation bias, media habits, and bias in AI systems.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: Representation and Stereotypes

Media representation affects how people understand groups, communities, events, and social issues. Repeated stereotypes can shape public perception, especially when certain groups are shown in limited or negative ways.

Ask Yourself

  • Who is shown?
  • Who is left out?
  • How are different groups described?
  • Are people being reduced to stereotypes?

Lesson 2: Bias in AI Systems and Algorithms

AI systems learn from data. If the data contains bias, the AI system may repeat or amplify that bias. Bias can appear in search results, facial recognition, recommendations, automated decisions, and AI-generated responses.

Examples

  • Facial recognition working less accurately for some groups
  • Search results reinforcing stereotypes
  • Recommendation systems promoting one type of viewpoint
  • AI tools giving unfair or incomplete answers

Lesson 3: Confirmation Bias and Media Habits

Confirmation bias happens when people pay more attention to information that supports what they already believe. Algorithms can strengthen this by recommending similar content.

A strong habit is to read reliable sources that challenge your first reaction, not only sources that agree with you.

Lesson 4: How Media Shapes Public Perception

Media can shape how people understand groups and events through repeated framing, selective examples, images, headlines, expert choices, and missing context.

Lesson 5: Real-World Examples of Bias

Bias can appear when articles quote only one side, when images make one group look threatening, when algorithms recommend extreme content, or when AI systems give different quality answers about different communities.

Activity:

Read two reliable sources about the same issue. Which one do you trust more? Why? Are you reacting to evidence, framing, or your own existing beliefs?

Quick Check:

How can bias appear in AI?

Answer: AI can repeat bias found in the data it was trained on.

Module 5

Case Studies

Apply your skills to viral misinformation, manipulated media, major events, and AI ethics.

Open detailed lessons

Lesson 1: Viral Misinformation Campaigns

Viral misinformation spreads quickly because people react emotionally and share before checking. The faster something spreads, the harder it can be to correct.

Analyze It

  • What claim is being made?
  • Who shared it first?
  • What emotions does it trigger?
  • What evidence supports or challenges it?

Lesson 2: Manipulated or Misleading Media

Images and videos can be cropped, edited, staged, taken out of context, or generated by AI. A real image can become misleading when paired with a false caption.

Media Literacy Move

  • Look for the original image or video.
  • Check when and where it was first posted.
  • Compare it with reports from trusted sources.
  • Be careful with screenshots that do not show context.

Lesson 3: Social Media and Major Events

During major events, social media can spread useful updates, but it can also spread rumors, fake images, old videos, and misleading claims.

Before sharing, check the date, location, source, and whether reliable outlets confirm the information.

Lesson 4: Ethical Issues Involving AI and Digital Media

AI can generate fake evidence, impersonate people, produce persuasive misinformation, or create content without clear disclosure. Ethical analysis asks who is harmed, who benefits, what consent exists, and whether the audience is being misled.

Lesson 5: Media Literacy Project Workflow

  1. Pause before reacting.
  2. Identify the main claim.
  3. Check the source.
  4. Check the evidence.
  5. Inspect visuals or graphs.
  6. Compare with independent sources.
  7. Decide your confidence level.
  8. Decide whether it should be shared.

Confidence labels can include verified, likely true, plausible but unconfirmed, misleading, unsupported, likely false, or not enough evidence.

Scenario Practice:

A viral post contains a dramatic headline, a chart with no source, a realistic image, a quote from an unnamed expert, and a link to a low-transparency website.

  1. What are the red flags?
  2. What should you verify first?
  3. What confidence label would you give it?
  4. Would you share it?
Quick Check:

Which statement shows strong media literacy?

Answer: “The claim is plausible, but I need stronger evidence before sharing it.”
